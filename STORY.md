# Whispers — Story Bible

Detailed narrative reference for the vertical slice. Production plan lives in [PLAN.md](./PLAN.md).

---

## The Player

Blank. Anonymous. No name, no gender, no backstory, no memory of how they arrived in the forest. The player projects onto them.

The amnesia is not a writing convenience — it is the horror. The player doesn't know who they were because *not knowing* is part of what the place took from them. By the end, the question isn't whether they escape. It's whether they were ever the person they thought they were at all.

---

## The Place

A forest that shouldn't exist. Dusk light that doesn't change. Trees that are *almost* the kind you know. Fog that doesn't move.

The place **was built**. Not grown, not wild — built, by something with a will. Its purpose: to collect voices. Specifically, to harvest the voice of someone trying to escape — because the trying is what feeds whatever is underneath.

The place lets victims wander, hope, and bargain. It needs them to believe they can leave. The moment they believe hard enough to act on it — to sacrifice someone, to accept the deal — it takes them.

The place does not change. The place does not learn. It has done this many, many times.

---

## The Entity

The antagonist. The thing that built the place, or the thing the place belongs to. The distinction doesn't matter.

### Face

The Entity has **its own true face** — distinctly wrong, creepy, the kind children would draw and adults would dismiss as a nightmare. It almost never shows this face. It wears masks instead.

The masks are **the faces of the dead**. Specific victims. The player hears a whisper mention a sister → the Entity appears wearing the sister's face. The player hears about a child → the Entity wears the child's face for a while.

At the climax, the Entity wears **the player's own face**. That is the moment the player understands what the Entity is.

### Manner

Cycling, unpredictable — but **never warm**. Three modes it rotates between:

- **Cold.** Polite, flat, customer-service-for-the-afterlife. Says horrific things in a business tone. ("The forest closes at dusk. You'll need to decide before then.")
- **Mocking.** Icy wit. Finds the player's hope funny. ("You still think there's a road out there. That's my favorite part.")
- **Rage.** Brief, terrifying flashes. Drops the mask entirely — including the literal one. Shows a sliver of its true face. Then composes itself. The player never knows when this will happen.

The absence of warmth is the tell. Nothing that should be on the player's side is ever actually kind. The player's instinct that *something is wrong here* is correct from the first scene.

### Name

**None.** It has no name and refuses to give one, or claims it forgot. Things without names are worse than things with names. The player refers to it internally as *it*. The whispers also call it *it* — they never named it either.

### What it claims

The Entity tells the player it is **also trapped**. Says it has been here longer than the whispers. Says the whispers are the trap, not the forest. Says if the player listens to the whispers, the player will end up like them — a voice with no body.

This is **a lie**. But it's a plausible lie, because the whispers ARE unreliable. The player cannot confirm which source to trust. That is the central paranoia.

### What it wants

The Entity needs the player to **choose to sacrifice someone**. The choice is what feeds the place. If the player simply dies or gives up, the Entity gets nothing. It needs the player's agency.

So the Entity is patient. It is willing to wait. It will guide, mock, threaten — whatever it takes to push the player toward the choice.

## The Whispers

### What they are

A chorus of the dead — previous victims who accepted the Entity's deal, sacrificed someone, and *became whispers without realizing it.* They think they escaped. They are still here.

They do not know they are dead. They do not know their warnings failed. They are trying to save the player from a trap they themselves already fell into.

### Cast

**Many voices, no individual identities.** The chorus acts as a single shifting character. The player never learns names, never meets a distinct "whisperer." Voices overlap, finish each other's sentences, contradict each other within a single breath.

This is intentional — individual victims would be characters you could empathize with. The faceless chorus is something you can drown in.

### What they did in life

**They all sacrificed someone.** That is why they are here. They brought someone else into the forest to escape.

But they **cannot or will not tell the player this.** Their guilt is silent. They never confess, never explain, never warn the player away from the specific choice they themselves made. They speak around the wound, never to it.

### What they sound like

**Unrelated memories.** Each whisper cutscene is a fragment of someone's life that seems to have nothing to do with anything:

- A description of a birthday cake. The candles. The wish.
- A song. Half of it. The chorus, not the verse.
- A face. Someone's face, described in fragments. Never named.
- The smell of a hospital hallway.
- The weight of a sleeping child.

These memories **seem unrelated** — until the player reaches the ending and the pattern resolves. (See *The Click*, below.)

### What they do NOT do

- They never say "run."
- They never say "trust it" or "don't trust it."
- They never explain the place.
- They never confess what they did.
- They never warn the player directly about the sacrifice.

They cannot. The place took that from them when it took their voices.

## The Objects

Interactable items in the forest that trigger whisper cutscenes. Each is an anchor for fragments.

| Object | Location | What it triggers |
|---|---|---|
| A child's shoe | First clearing | The first whisper cutscene. A fragment about lullabies. |
| A torn photograph | Beneath a specific tree | Fragments about a face — someone's face, half-remembered. |
| A name carved in bark | Deep in the forest | Fragments about naming, being named, calling someone by name. |
| A music box | Abandoned campsite | Fragments about a song — the same song the lullaby referenced. |
| A jacket, folded neatly | Final clearing before the turn | Fragments about the weight of someone sleeping. The heaviest cutscene. |

**The child's shoe is never explained.** No whisperer claims it. It is just there — a thing the place left as bait, or residue from a sacrifice long absorbed. The player will spend the game assuming it matters. It does not. That is the point.

---

## The Shifts

Brief (10–30 sec) moments where the forest dissolves into a different location. The player walks through, then returns. The Entity follows, never acknowledging the shift happened.

**Dreamlike. Disorienting. No clear logic.** Specific shifts in the slice:

1. **A hospital hallway** that loops. The player walks past the same nurses' station three times. The Entity walks with them, smiling.
2. **A childhood bedroom.** The player is taller than they should be. The wallpaper is wrong. The bed is made.

The shifts are not flashbacks. They are not the whisperers' memories. They are the place **showing the player something the player has forgotten about themselves** — but only as wallpaper, only as atmosphere, never as information the player can hold onto.

## The Click (the ending's revelation)

Across the game, the whispers have offered fragments:
- a lullaby
- a face
- a name
- a song
- the weight of a sleeping child

At the ending, after the player has accepted or refused the Entity's deal, the fragments resolve. The player realizes:

**The memories were all of the same person.** A child. The child the player once had. The child the player brought to the forest, long ago, in exchange for their own escape.

The player forgot. The place took the memory along with everything else. The whispers have been the player's own memories, surfacing in the voices of people who did the same thing.

The player is not a victim. **The player is the latest whisperer.** They have been here longer than they think. They have done this before. The "amnesiac waking in the forest" is not the start of their story — it is a loop. They woke here because they woke here because they woke here.

The Entity knew. The Entity has been waiting for them to remember just enough to choose again. The choice is the loop's engine.

### The final cinematic

1. The player accepts or refuses the Entity's offer.
2. Either way, the forest opens. A road. City lights. The player walks toward them — relief, joy, the audience thinking the player has escaped.
3. The whispers begin. The player's own voice is among them.
4. The player hears themselves describing a lullaby. A face. A name. A song. The weight of a sleeping child.
5. The player's face appears on the Entity — the mask it wears now.
6. Cut to: another person, anonymous, faceless, waking in the forest. They look around, confused.
7. The whispers greet them.
8. Title card.

## Open Creative Questions

- **The Entity's true face design.** Needs 3 sketch concepts before milestone M5. Suggested direction: asymmetrical, too many teeth, eyes that don't track the player.
- **Specific whisper lines.** 12–15 fragments need to be written. Each should feel like a memory, not a warning.
- **The Entity's dialogue.** 8–10 lines across the game. Cold mode mostly, one or two mocking, one rage flash.
- **Language.** English or Turkish? — pick one for the slice.
- **The Entity's voice performance.** Flat and polite (recommended) vs. whispered vs. distorted.
- **Does the player realize before the credits?** Recommended: no. The realization happens TO them in the final cinematic. The player of the game pieces it together slightly ahead of the character — that gap is the tragedy.
